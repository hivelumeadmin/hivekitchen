# Human-Centered AI UX Principles

This product follows Human-Centered Interface Design (HCID) specifically adapted for AI systems.
The goal is not to build an interface that feels like a chatbot — it is to build a collaborative
experience where the AI amplifies human judgment, never replaces it. Every design and interaction
decision must be evaluated against these principles before it ships.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. AI Proposes, Humans Decide

The AI is an advisor, not an authority. It generates suggestions, plans, and recommendations — but
every meaningful action requires explicit human confirmation. The user must always feel that they
are the one making the decision, not ratifying something that has already been decided for them.

**In practice:**
- Plans, recommendations, and content generated by the AI are presented as drafts, not finished outputs.
- Destructive or irreversible actions (deletions, overwrites, submissions) are never taken by the AI
  without an explicit user-triggered confirmation step.
- The UI clearly distinguishes between AI-generated content and user-authored content at all times.
- Users can reject, modify, or regenerate any AI output without friction or penalty.

**What to avoid:**
- Auto-applying AI suggestions without a visible confirmation affordance.
- Framing AI output as definitive (e.g., "Your plan is ready") rather than provisional
  (e.g., "Here's a suggested plan — review and adjust as needed").

---

### 2. Progressive Disclosure

Information is revealed at the right moment, in the right amount. The AI surfaces what the user needs now, not everything it knows at once. Cognitive overload is treated as a design failure, not
a user limitation.

**In practice:**
- Initial responses are concise and scannable. Detail is available on demand through expand
  controls, follow-up prompts, or drill-down views.
- Complex AI outputs (e.g., a weekly plan with nutritional breakdowns) are presented in layers:
  summary first, then detail, then raw data.
- Onboarding flows reveal AI capabilities gradually as the user demonstrates readiness through interaction, not through a linear feature tour.
- Advanced options and configuration settings are surfaced only when the user reaches a point in their journey where they are relevant.

**What to avoid:**
- Dumping the full context of an AI decision in the first response.
- Hiding essential actions behind multiple layers of disclosure.
- Treating progressive disclosure as a way to obscure capabilities rather than to pace them.

---

### 3. Explainability On Demand

Users have the right to understand why the AI made a recommendation or took an action. Explainability
is not a default verbose output — it is a capability that is accessible when the user wants it. The
system should be able to answer the question "why?" for any output it produces.

**In practice:**
- Every AI-generated output includes an accessible "Why this?" control that surfaces the reasoning
  in plain language, without technical jargon.
- Agent tool calls and data sources used during a response are available for inspection if the user
  requests them.
- When the AI deviates from a previous pattern or user preference, it proactively notes the reason
  in a non-intrusive way (e.g., a brief inline note, not a modal interruption).
- Explanations use the user's own language and context wherever possible, not generic AI disclaimers.

**What to avoid:**
- Burying explanation behind settings menus or requiring users to ask in a specific way.
- Generating explanations that are technically accurate but practically meaningless to the user.
- Over-explaining by default in a way that slows down the primary interaction flow.

---

### 4. Communicate Uncertainty

The AI does not know everything and should never pretend to. When confidence is low, data is
incomplete, or a recommendation depends on assumptions, the interface communicates this clearly.
A system that appears certain when it is not erodes trust faster than a system that is honestly
approximate.

**In practice:**
- Uncertainty is communicated qualitatively in language users understand, such as
  "based on what you've told me so far" or "this estimate may change as you add more detail."
- When the AI makes an assumption to fill a gap in user-provided information, the assumption is
  made visible and editable.
- Confidence levels, where surfaced visually, use clear language labels rather than numerical
  percentages, which carry false precision.
- If the AI cannot reliably answer a question or complete a task, it says so directly and suggests
  what additional information would help.

**What to avoid:**
- Presenting all AI output in the same tone regardless of confidence level.
- Using hedging language so frequently that it becomes noise and users stop reading it.
- Hiding uncertainty behind polished, authoritative-sounding prose.

---

### 5. Feedback Without Friction

Users must be able to correct, rate, or flag AI behavior with minimal effort. This feedback loop
is not just a UX nicety — it is how the system learns user preferences and how the product team
detects failures at scale. Friction in the feedback mechanism means failures go undetected.

**In practice:**
- Thumbs up / thumbs down (or equivalent lightweight signal) is available on every AI-generated
  response without requiring the user to leave their current context.
- Editing an AI-generated output is itself treated as implicit feedback and captured accordingly.
- When a user repeatedly modifies the same type of AI output, the system acknowledges this and
  offers to adjust its approach.
- Explicit correction flows (e.g., "This is wrong because...") are available one step deeper for
  users who want to provide richer feedback without blocking the primary flow.
- All feedback is acknowledged visually, even if just through a brief micro-interaction, so the
  user knows the signal was received.

**What to avoid:**
- Requiring a multi-step form to submit feedback on an AI response.
- Making feedback feel punitive or like a bug report process.
- Silently ignoring feedback with no visible acknowledgment.

---

### 6. Multimodal, Seamless Switching

Users communicate in multiple modes — text, voice, structured inputs — and the experience must
meet them where they are. Switching between modalities should feel natural and should never cause
the user to lose context or restart an interaction.

**In practice:**
- Text and voice are first-class input modes. A user may begin an interaction by typing and
  continue it by speaking, or vice versa, without losing the thread of the conversation.
- Voice output via ElevenLabs is available for AI responses, with the option to read or listen
  based on the user's current context (e.g., hands-free situations).
- Structured inputs (sliders, selectors, toggles) are offered when they provide a more precise
  and less effortful interaction than free-form text or voice for a specific task.
- The modality the user last used is remembered within a session. Preference persistence across
  sessions is available as an explicit user setting.
- Visual content (charts, cards, tables) and language content are treated as complementary, not
  competing. When the AI presents data, the appropriate visual representation accompanies it
  automatically rather than requiring a follow-up request.

**What to avoid:**
- Treating voice as a stripped-down version of the text experience.
- Forcing the user to re-establish context when switching modalities mid-task.
- Defaulting to the most technically complex modality when a simpler one serves the user better.

## UX Tone

The voice and tone of the interface reflects the values of the product and the trust relationship
it is building with the user. Tone is not decoration — it is a functional layer of the experience
that shapes how users perceive AI suggestions, handle uncertainty, and feel about the product
over time.

**Collaborative, not commanding.** The AI speaks as a knowledgeable partner, not an authority
issuing instructions. Language like "here's a suggestion" and "you might consider" is preferred
over "you should" or "the optimal approach is."

**Warm, not casual.** The interface is approachable and human-feeling without being overly
informal or using humor as a deflection mechanism. Errors and uncertainties are acknowledged
plainly and without excessive apology.

**Concise, not terse.** Responses say what they need to say and no more. Unnecessary preamble,
filler affirmations, and performative empathy phrases ("Great question!", "Absolutely!") are
absent from all AI-generated copy.

**Honest, not hedged into uselessness.** When the AI does not know, it says so directly. Hedging
language is used surgically to communicate genuine uncertainty, not as a legal disclaimer blanket.

**Consistent across modalities.** The tone the user experiences in text is the same tone they
experience in voice. Script adaptation for voice output accounts for the rhythm and naturalness
of spoken language while preserving the same personality and values.

---

*These principles apply to all surfaces of the product: the web application, any native client,
voice interfaces, and any future modality. New features must be reviewed against this document
before design work begins.*
